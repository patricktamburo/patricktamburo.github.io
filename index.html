Current Projects

Searching for Transiting Planets around Brown Dwarfs

Brown dwarfs are sub-stellar objects, meaning they don't have enough mass to sustain hydrogen fusion. They are much smaller and cooler than our own Sun: a planet in the habitable zone around a brown dwarf would need to have an orbital period of days! They are very small objects, roughly the size of Jupiter, and this amplifies the signals from transiting planets, those that pass in front of their host from our line of sight. This facilitates the discovery of small, rocky planets, which provide a solid surface from which life may develop. The atmospheres of these planets will be potentially observable with the upcoming James Webb Space Telescope, which may enable the first detection of an Earth-like atmosphere outside of our solar system.

However, brown dwarfs have gone largely untargeted in exoplanet transit surveys, mainly because they are faint at optical wavelengths. We are currently planning a search for transiting planets around northern hemisphere brown dwarfs at infrared wavelengths, where they are much brighter. We plan to execute this survey using the 1.8-m Perkins Telescope in Flagstaff, AZ, and over the course of a three-year effort, we expect to discover two Earth-sized planets.

Archiving Cassini Electron Density Profiles

The Cassini mission took 60 radio occultation observations of Saturn's ionsphere, measuring electron density as a function of height. These measurements have provided new insights into the planet's upper atmosphere, including an unexpected increase in peak electron density with increasing latitude. We are working to archive these profiles on the NASA Planetary Data System so they can be used by other researchers studying the Saturn system.
